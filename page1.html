The politics of design

The main focus of this project is to illustrate that design is inherently polarizing: it governs not only how things look but also how things work. On this page, I will detail the systemic operations and psychological processes that underpin human-computer interaction specifically in social media spaces and with regard to disinformation.

LET'S GET STARTED

Why should we care about design in the disinformation ecosystem?

Design is a quiet but central player in—well—everything! Most people don't recognize that their actions are being mediated through design because we don't always see that it's there (until it breaks or is frustrating to navigate, but that would be bad design!).

There are two key ways of understanding a design object: the architectural model and the affordance model. 

Architectual Model: Structural

The organization of features, buttons, and tangible tools. In tech, this is referred to as 'information architecture,' or IA for short. IA is responsible for the organization, representation of, and user navigation of information.

Affordance Model: Behavioral

The perception of what actions a platform or individual feature enables. It also describes the relationship and subjectivities of a user to the product or artifact. In other words, affordances are the potential outcomes in a subject-object interaction.

The Structural & Behavioral
are Intimately Related

For example, take the design of this supermarket. The arrangement of product types (the deli section, produce, baking, etc.) refers to the architectural form of the space, its structure. Now, given the spacial arrangement, what types of behaviors—or affordances—are enabled?

Let's say you took a trip to the supermarket to restock on some delicious almond milk. To get to the 'dairy-and-friends' section, you are forced to walk to the very back, past all those chips you've been craving! Defeated, you grab those sea salt & vinegar chips on your way out.

This scenario demonstrates two things: the first being that the structural dictates the behavioral. The placement of the almond milk made you walk to the back of the store, pretty obvious. Less obvious are the distinct agendas that informed the placement of said almond milk. The architect of this space wanted you to walk to the back so that you would see more products along the way, in hopes that you would make additional impulse buys.

Thus, the second point this scenario shows is that design is not neutral. A designer makes calculated decisions based on what they want the user to see, or more nefariously, not see. 

Now think of your preferred social media platform and think about its architecture.

What steps do you have to take in order to do something?
What things are you forced to see along the way?
And why do you think that is?

We don't always go on social media with a clear task in mind like we would going into a grociery store,
but the same relationship between the structural and the behavioral still apply.

More complicated than the architecture of a grociery store, social media
often employs algorithms that change the structural organization of content every time you log in.
As many people by now know, you don't see content in the order they were published,
but by a complicated system that is attempting to personalize your information environment.

With that, social media obviously introduces a new psychological arena based on user motivations,
rather than clear objectives (most of the time, anyways).

Main Takeaway No.1:

The Structural-Behavioral Relationship

Affordances are determined by the design object's architectural realization. By this logic, design is not neutral, but instead reflects a carefully calibrated ideology.

The Psychology of Social Media

Digital media consumption is a form of information processing, a largely psychological arena. When one is presented with information, dual-process models of judgment formation posit that the brain processes information one of two ways:

Centrally

Here, we thoughtfully scrutinize the arguments and evidence presented. This is a conscious process that is sometimes referred to as "true reasoning," where reasoning is used to solve problems. An example where we use central judgement processing is when we use a map to navigate ourselves physically. 

Peripherally

Where judgement is based on emotional, visual, and social cues. This is often an unconscious process that is grounded in association. When we analyze information peripherally, we are trying to provide ourselves ideas we're familiar with to compare to what we're confronted with.

Whether a person processes content centrally or peripherally is contingent on his/her motivation and ability to do so. The social media environment itself (with huge quantities of bite-sized information, constantly updated in real time) encourages peripheral processing by placing constraints on both users’ ability and motivation to think carefully about the information presented. 

When you come across this tweet, what information do you pick up on before performing your next action (scroll past, read the full article, retweet, etc.)? Do you tend to ask yourself any of the following questions? Again, it's not that everyone processes this way, only that this logic is encouraged architecturally.

1

Who is publishing?

2

What does the headline suggest? Can I take any information from the headline alone?

3

Did someone share this? Who? What do I think about this person?

4

How many people liked/commented/shared?

Main Takeaway No.2:

The Perihperal Paradigm

Social media tends to appeal to peripheral information processing. Information is presented in a way that encourages users to pay attention to quick, bite-sized hooks rather than careful, analytical reading.

What does peripheral processing have to do with disinformation?

It's actually quite the dilemma. With respect to news-sharing, users who have a propensity for peripheral processing don't usually read the stories, but instead grasp on to sensationalized headlines and 'soundbites.' 

Like I brought up earlier, it also causes these users to judge the value of information relative to the person who is speaking it. This is really exacerbated by algorithms because it will prioritize content from accounts it presumes you want to see from. 

While the conditions for an echo chamber are exceedingly rare, empirical evidence shows that the more one’s media environment looks like an echo chamber, the more extreme one’s viewpoints are. 

But this begs the question: do people need a "balanced diet" of information? Personally I don't feel that exposing people to both well-researched and misinformed content is not healthy to democracy, and an extended concept from peripheral processing has led me to this position:

Continued Influence Effect (CIE)

This occurs when information that initially is presumed to be correct, but that is later retracted or corrected; this often continues to influence memory and reasoning. This occurs even if the retraction itself is well remembered.

Let's say before you leave to the market you come across this article 

There is an ingredient claimed by some scientists found to cause inflammation and digestive issues commonly found in some brands of almond milk.

So you quickly scourage the internet for an alternative to your alternative milk

There are actually quite a few brands that don't use the suspect ingradient and that are found virtually anywhere you can get almond milk—great! Unfortunately, they cost about $1.50 more than the brand you used to buy...

Later, there is evidence to suggest a counter claims of the original article, but you still don't buy that it's totally safe; do you?

You were led to believe something from a credible source with scientific evidence, so why should you just drop everything you thought you knew about this ingredient? You're probably just going to stick to avoiding it, aren't you?

So given the persistence of CIE in media environments, what can design do for us?

I believe the first step pinpointing an opportunity for design to work in social media/digital information spaces is to create a quasi-journey map of encountering (dis)information. This way, we can see where is the best opportunity to intervene.

1

Pre-Encounter Media Space

What leads up to scrolling to the information? Are there tools the prevent you from encountering specific agencies beforehand?

2

Real-time Architectural Space

What tools are at your disposal in the event you encounter the (dis)information?

3

Post-Encounter Measures

Are there tools provided for you to reduce your own CIE or to stop the (dis)information from persisting? What do you do after reading?

A 2010 study  investigated whether the continued influence of misinformation can be reduced by explicitly warning people at the outset that they may be misled.

A specific warning—giving detailed information about the CIE—succeeded in reducing the continued reliance on outdated information but did not eliminate it. A more general warning—reminding people that facts are not always properly checked before information is disseminated—was even less effective.

Thus, the best way to reduce the effects of CIE is to employ specific warnings. The space that requires the most strategic design work is the 'pre-encounter' space; but that doesn't mean designers shouldn't think critically about real-time and post-encounters in the context of the current disinformation ecosystem.

Main Takeaway No.3:

The CIE Complex

Continued Influence Effect (CIE) occurs when information that initially is presumed to be correct, but that is later retracted or corrected. The best way to reduce the effect of CIE is through explicit warnings in pre-encounter media spaces.

Designing for Credibility

How one makes sense of their information environment differs from person to person. However, there are very specific psychological tropes that designers can recognize when designing for credibility; those tropes being:

  1. How Information is Presented Matters: the quantity of information directly affects how information is consumed (i.e. repetition perceived as truth, echo chambers affirm biases).
  2. Information Processing is Connected to Social Identity: How we see ourselves, as well as how we want others to see us, both profoundly shape what information we choose to read, believe, and share. We seek out information that affirms our social identity and avoid information that undermines it.
  3. The Peripheral Paradigm: As a result, factors such as “who shared it” and “does this make me feel good/right” become more likely to dictate processing goals and dissemination behaviors.
